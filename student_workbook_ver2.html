Electrical & Electronics Fundamentals

Everything you measure later — on a control panel or an EV battery — starts here: voltage, current, resistance, and the safe way to work with them.

Week 1

Circuit Basics: Ohm's Law & Series/Parallel Circuits

Goal: Be able to calculate voltage, current, or resistance in any simple circuit, and know the difference between series and parallel wiring.
Key Concepts

Every circuit runs on three connected quantities: voltage (V), current (I), and resistance (R). Ohm's Law ties them together: V = I × R. If you know any two, you can always find the third.

  • In a series circuit, current is the same everywhere; voltage divides across each component.
  • In a parallel circuit, voltage is the same across every branch; current divides between branches.
🔧 Try it: Open the Electrical & Control Practice Panel — Exercise 1 and solve at least 6 practice problems.
Check Your Understanding
1. If V = 24V and R = 6Ω, what is I?
I = V ÷ R = 24 ÷ 6 = 4A
2. Two 20Ω resistors are wired in parallel. What is the total resistance?
R_total = (20×20)/(20+20) = 400/40 = 10Ω

Week 2

Electrical Safety Procedure & Protective Equipment

Goal: Know the 5-step isolation sequence from memory, and be able to judge whether protective equipment is safe to use.
Key Concepts

Before touching a live circuit, follow the same sequence every time: power off → gloves on → disconnect the isolation point → wait for stored energy to discharge → confirm 0V with a meter.

  • The waiting step matters because components like capacitors can hold energy even after power is cut.
  • Protective gloves must be inspected before use and replaced on a fixed schedule.
🔧 Try it: Open Exercise 2 — Isolation Sequence & PPE and get 3 flawless runs in a row. Watch the small 3D panel above the cards light up as you pick each step.
Check Your Understanding
1. Why is the "wait" step included even if the circuit looks off?
Stored energy (e.g., in capacitors) can remain even after power is cut — skipping the wait risks shock.
2. What confirms a circuit is truly safe to touch?
A meter reading of 0V — not just "looking" off.

Week 3

Reading a Meter: Insulation Resistance

Goal: Read a measured value, compare it to a threshold, and judge normal vs. suspect — a pattern you'll use for the rest of this program.
Key Concepts

An insulation resistance tester measures how well an insulator blocks current. A common rule of thumb: minimum acceptable resistance is 100Ω per volt of system voltage.

  • A number alone means nothing — you always need a reference (threshold) to compare it to.
  • A reading close to or below the threshold is a warning sign, even if it isn't proof of failure.
🔧 Try it: Open Exercise 3 — Insulation Resistance Judgment and answer at least 6 readings.
Check Your Understanding
1. At 400V, what is the minimum acceptable insulation resistance?
400 × 100Ω = 40,000Ω = 40kΩ
2. Should a borderline reading be treated as pass or suspect?
Suspect — re-check rather than assuming a pass.

PLC & Control Basics

Automated systems run on a loop: sense → decide → act. This stage teaches you to name the parts of that loop, read the logic behind it, and practice the judgment it demands.

Week 4

Sensors, Actuators & Control Basics

Goal: Correctly identify sensors vs. actuators, and describe a simple control loop end to end.
Key Concepts
  • Sensor = detects a condition and turns it into a signal.
  • Actuator = takes a command and produces physical action.
  • Controller = reads sensor signals, applies logic, and commands actuators.

1. Is a push-button a sensor or an actuator?
A sensor — it turns a human action into a signal a controller can read.

Week 5

Introduction to Ladder Logic

Goal: Read a basic ladder logic rung and tell whether it represents an AND or an OR condition.
Key Concepts
  • Ladder logic shows control decisions as horizontal "rungs."
  • Contacts in series = AND. Contacts in parallel = OR.
  • Reading left to right tells you exactly what must be true for the output to turn on.

Check Your Understanding
1. "Turn on the fan if EITHER the temperature sensor OR the manual switch is active" — series or parallel?
Parallel (OR).

Week 6

Alarms, Interlocks & Reading an HMI

Goal: Correctly classify a monitored value as normal/warning/danger and choose a matching response.
Key Concepts
  • Alarm = a signal that a value has crossed a threshold.
  • Interlock = a safety condition that prevents an action until satisfied.
  • Read colour and the number together — never colour alone.
🔧 Try it: Open Exercise 6 — Alarm & HMI Reading and complete at least 5 readings.
Check Your Understanding
1. Why can't you rely on colour alone when reading a screen?
Colour tells you the category; the number tells you how close to the edge you are. You need both.

Week 9

Battery SOC & SOH Concepts

Goal: Explain the difference between SOC and SOH, and why a battery can show "100% charged" but still have reduced real capacity.
Key Concepts
  • SOC (State of Charge) = how full the battery is right now.
  • SOH (State of Health) = how much capacity it has left compared to new.
  • Dashboard SOC is an estimate, not a direct measurement, so it can drift.
🔧 Try it: Open the EV Fundamentals Digital Twin — SOC/SOH Lab, try both presets, then answer the Concept Check quiz below the sliders.
Check Your Understanding
1. If SOC = 100% and SOH = 70%, roughly what percentage of a new battery's real capacity are you getting?
About 70% (100% × 70% = 70%).

Week 10

AC/DC Charging Basics

Goal: Explain the structural difference between AC and DC fast charging, and reason about whether a charging fault is likely vehicle-side or charger-side.
Key Concepts
  • AC charging: the vehicle's own On-Board Charger (OBC) converts AC to DC before it reaches the battery.
  • DC fast charging: the charger has already converted to DC and supplies it directly, bypassing the OBC.
  • A fault that repeats across multiple chargers points to the vehicle; a fault that only happens on one charger points to the charger.
🔧 Try it: Open the EV Fundamentals Digital Twin — Charging Flow. Toggle between AC and DC, complete the "Sequence the AC Power Flow" drill, then answer a few rounds of the Fault Classification Quiz.
Check Your Understanding
1. Why is DC fast charging generally quicker than AC charging?
It skips the vehicle's own AC-to-DC conversion step and delivers already-converted power directly.
